aesthocratic
/ ˌiːs.θəˈkræt.ɪk /
adjective · formal, chiefly critical theory
Etymology

From Greek aisthesis (perception, sensation; from aisthanesthai, to perceive) + -kratia (rule, governance; from kratos, strength, power). Formed on the model of technocratic and bureaucratic. Coined in 20th-century art and architectural criticism to describe institutions or spaces governed principally by considerations of taste.

First attested

Mid-20th century; earliest traceable use in a 1962 essay on postwar institutional design, where it was applied to public buildings prioritising visual prestige over usability. Subsequently adopted in cultural theory and design criticism.

Usage note

Formal register; found in art criticism, architectural commentary, and cultural theory. Used critically rather than approvingly. The word implies a systemic condition rather than a single aesthetic choice.

definition
  1. 1 Of a system, space, or institution in which aesthetic value governs over practical function. An aesthocratic environment looks exactly right and works exactly wrong.
  2. 2 (by extension) Of any decision-making process where the primacy of appearance or style consistently overrides utility or efficacy.
in use "The redesigned headquarters was entirely aesthocratic: striking to visit, impossible to work in."
