fonaligging
/ ˌfɒn.ə.ˈlɪɡ.ɪŋ /
present participle · dialectal, chiefly northern English
Etymology

Origin obscure; possibly from dialectal fonalig (to paw at something idly, recorded in West Riding glossaries c. 1840), itself of uncertain derivation. Compare Scottish fankling (to entangle without purpose) and Northumbrian fondle in its earlier, non-affectionate sense of handling loosely.

First attested

West Riding dialect glossaries, c. 1840, with the gloss "to handle without purpose." Possibly older in spoken use. Not recorded in literary sources; the word appears to have remained confined to informal speech.

Usage note

Almost exclusively dialectal; rarely appears outside speech or informal writing. The word carries no strong evaluative charge: fonaligging is observed rather than judged. It names the action precisely without implying restlessness or anxiety.

definition
  1. 1 To handle or fiddle with something absentmindedly, without intention or purpose. Fonaligging is distinguished from restlessness by its calm; there is no urgency in the motion, only idle, repeated contact.
  2. 2 (noun use, rare) The act or habit of such handling: "the fonaligging of a pen cap during long meetings."
in use "He spent the whole call fonaligging with the corner of a sticky note, folding and unfolding it."
